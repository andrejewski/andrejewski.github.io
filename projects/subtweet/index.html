Subtweet

I created a group messaging application on top of Twitter to combat the issues of their own Direct Messaging (DM) service. And here is the story of everything that happened.

To many of us in the developer community who use Twitter, it was unfathomable how Twitter had so greatly neglected their Direct Messaging service. There were a lot of problems with it and the roadmap for improvements was non-existent. This left open room for innovation for however temporary the window was open.

Subtweet was that innovation. Subtweet mimicked the general concepts of Twitter DMs and on top of it built group messaging, tweet embedding, public conversations, made conversation real-time (instead of using slow polling), never deleted old messages over time, and removed the character limit on messages. It used Twitter’s API for most everything and I did not see it as being anything but better Direct Messaging. As I used Subtweet alongside Twitter for conversations, Subtweet out performed in all aspects of messaging.

To build such an app, I used some new tools: React, Flux, and Superagent (bundled with Browserify). I did originally try to do server-side rendering but as the main app view got more complex I knew I would need to bring more functionality into the client for speed and smoothness. Out of my experience with React and Flux, I built my own Flux-architecture Even More Flux that I prefer in newer applications where React is needed instead of the bare architecture provided by Facebook.

Well-versed in the Twitter API, I was able to divert most of the work of authenticating users and loading embedded tweets to Twitter. Subtweet required a database only for user sessions and message storage, every other piece of information was imported from Twitter. Operation costs were low on my end and there was no need to keep local data fresh as I could tap Twitter to update user information behind the scenes.

This ease is, however, not Twitter’s intention. In order to offload the work to Twitter, I had to do a lot of hacking on their JavaScript library to get it to work as I wanted it. Working around the Twitter API was where most of my development time was spent. Luckily some brave souls had come before me and made open-source projects to expedite the authentication process, but for anything else I was on my own.

I designed Subtweet from the beginning to be a mobile-first application. This meant building all the functionality cleanly into the mobile experience and then growing out the design for the desktop experience. The intention was that people who most likely be communicating with their phones instead of going to their computer to check-in or respond. Without building a native application for both iOS and Android, a mobile-first webapp was the fastest and most cost-effective solution.

Twitter finally launched group messaging and now it seems Subtweet no longer has a strong use case. While Subtweet does have many features Twitter does not, other means of communication are more suitable for the use cases that remain. Subtweet shut down in the subsequent months after exporting conversation histories for interested users.
