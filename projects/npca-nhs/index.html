Northwest Pennsylvania Collegiate Academy National Honor Society

My high school Northwest Pennsylvania Collegiate Academy (NPCA) has a National Honor Society club. The members are junior and senior students and the senior class has elected officials that manage the club. There are only four electoral positions â€” president, vice president, secretary, and treasurer. Needless to say, a club such as NHS requires a lot of coordination. With more than 150 members, a lot of things needed to be improved, steam-lined, automated, and eliminated. That's where I came in.

Problems

The problems faced by the NHS officials were plentiful. News and announcements needed to be distributed via email, which was often a hit-or-miss and many students would not check their email account urgently enough to respond. Volunteering opportunities were disorderly with emails back and forth, handling which events were at capacity and which needed to be filled. There was a clear disconnect between the officials and the members.

Email was as technologically advanced as the club had progressed. To log service hours, members had to fill out complicated forms for each event they attended and some lost the hours they mistakenly forgot to record. This particular club required a certain number of service hours for any given member to remain in good standing.

Managing the announcements, the events, who was going, who did go, how many hours members had volunteered were very overwhelming problems. if I were to improve the NHS experience I needed to address all of these pain points.

Strategy

I began researching National Honor Society structure, organizations, and the tasks that revolved around a successful club.

The most apparent concept of NHS was the member hierarchy. The highest authority is the administrator, which is usually a teacher or member of the school's staff. Then there are the senior officers, followed by the committee heads, and finally all other members.

In order to represent this structure digitally, each member is given a role which allows or disallows permissions to components of the application, such as adding new members. Administrators can do everything, officers less so, and so on down the pyramid.

It was important to keep in mind the user base is primarily teenagers, messy, destructive, and potentially-corruptible teenagers. The security and permissions for any management system need to robust, but more so with a younger audience that might seek out exploitations.

The next step was member registration. In years past, this was simply collecting a name and email address. I had no desire to complicate this process. Unlike most registration however, no one could just "sign up." A registration system needed to be implemented.

Announcements and news only need to have a title, subtitle, and text. Administrators also should be allowed to draft announcements so they can preview and collaborate on them before they are published. Markdown would offer a great amount of flexibility to article text with the added benefit of plaintext just working without any formatting knowledge.

Volunteering events are a bit more tricky. Looking at the array of events the NHS club hosted or broadcast, I noticed a pattern of how often one event would have multiple times. Events needed to be structured to cater to multiple times whilst easily sharing a unified description.

Events are also interactive. Members attend events. There would need to be a way for a member to schedule their participation in an event. This would allow officers to see who is attending and better monitor attendance and service hours.

With a checklist of features and understanding of the NHS process, I moved on to the pretty part.

Design

The easiest part of the NHS system to design was the homepage. This page did not require much functionality. Any type of visitor, member or not, would most likely view this page. It just needed to welcome, assist navigation, and above all else look professional.

The actual application is much more minimal. To provide a greater focus on content such as announcements and event, and also to reduce visual clutter, it was important to keep screens direct and simple.

The colors used are based upon Collegiate's school colors of gold and blue. To increase the visibility and recognition of certain elements, I included one of many Octicons to the mix.

Each screen only provides primary information about one object at a time; there is no competition for engagement, which improves usability while reducing complexity.

Individual events have there own pages contain a description of the event and multiple event times. The button group below the description is only visible and usable by officers or administrators.

Event is one central event which contains multiple different event times. For example, the clubs likes having bi-weekly meetings with one meeting before and another meeting after school. Event times save the trouble of duplicating information and the muddling of information hierarchy.

The user profile screen is my favorite because it really speaks to the power technology can bring to organization and management. The profile display the user's name, title (I made mine the CTO), an editable biography. There record of all my combined services hours, what events I have attended, and what events I have scheduled to attend.

Why this is my favorite requires some context. To manually construct this service record previously, a member would have had to fill out at least five forms correctly and promptly and an officer would need to tabulate and verify these forms to sum up the hours. Multiply that by 200 and hair pulling results. The man-hours wasted is a tragedy.

The NHS application handles all of these things, there no more forms to fill out and no one needs a calculator on standby. This user profile renders in a fraction of a second and updates just as fast. That is simply powerful.

There's one last thing. It is important to remember the user base is comprised of teenagers. Teenagers are on their mobile devices more than any other age group. To not have a mobile version of the NHS application would be unjustifiable.

Yes, the application is desktop, tablet, and mobile friendly with zero complicated redirects or other distractions.

Development

I divided the whole project into two parts. The website was hand-coded with Jade, the resultant HTML, and CSS. This work is really just the homepage and the mission statement page as some much of the project is the real application.

The application is written in Node.js using Express 4 to provide the excellently performant and modular routing logic. All communication with the database is handled by the Mongoose ORM. The database is MongoDB and configuration and setting files are JSON that I exposed asynchronously so they could change without breaking the running application.

The components of the application are separated into distinct folders contain each objects routes, model, and helpers. Marked handles converting text to HTML. Passport handles the authentication of members. Nodemailer handles registration and password resets for members. All of these dependencies are managed with the always reliable NPM.

The website and application happily coexist proxied behind Nginx. This measure is taken to ensure that even if the application crashes or takes on too much traffic the website will still serve properly.

Conclusion

I can sleep soundly at night knowing I have saved teenagers from having to fill out a few more forms in their lives. I hope my work improves the management, organization, and success of the National Honor Society of Northwest Pennsylvania Collegiate Academy.
